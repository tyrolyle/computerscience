INTRODUCTION AND ITS HISTORY

COMPUTER:

 A Computer is a electronic device used to process data and produce it processed as output. In early days, before the invention of electronic computers, people who did computations where known as computer.

 

HISTORY OF COMPUTERS:

By seeing about the computer history it was a fascinating history from mechanical calculator to the computer what we use today.

Let us move by timeline:

Early Calculating Devices (17th-19th Century): (analog)

In early days, ABACUS is used to do arithmetic operations, it is said to be the beginning of the calculating devices.

Later on SLIDE RULE- a type of mechanical analog computer used in the calculation of multiplication and division  

In 1822 and 1833, DIFFERENCE ENGINE and ANALYTICAL ENGINE designed by CHARLES BABBAGE, these two are mechanical calculators which is said to be the precursors of the modern day computer. It was not full developed but laid foundation for further development.

The Birth of Electronic Computers (1940 s-1950 s): (electronic)

ENIAC (1945):  The Electronic Numerical Integrator And Computer.  first general purpose electronic computer. Built during world war two. It used vacuum tubes. so that they can change position much faster than mechanical one. It could add 5,000 numbers in just one second, an astounding feat for the time. It took up an entire room and contained 18,000 vacuum tubes. Programming the ENIAC was much different than what software developers do today; this early work consisted of cables being plugged into plug boards.

UNIVAC I (1951): The Universal Automatic Computer I (UNIVAC I) was the first commercial computer available to the public. It was used for various purposes, including business record-keeping and scientific calculations.

The Transistor Revolution and Beyond (1950 s-Present):

Transistors: The invention of the transistor in the 1950 s revolutionized computers. Transistors are much smaller, faster, and more reliable than vacuum tubes, leading to the development of smaller, more efficient computers. Compared to vacuum tubes, transistors could be much smaller and used less power.  Transistors were developed at Bell Labs in New Jersey. William Shockley, John Bardeen, and Walter Brattain worked to create the transistor.The EDVAC was designed as an improvement of the ENIAC and could use stored programs to function.

Integrated Circuits (ICs): In the 1960 s, the invention of integrated circuits (ICs), also known as microchips, further miniaturized computers. ICs contain millions of transistors on a single chip, making it possible to build even more powerful computers in smaller packages.Creating and discovering computer technologies has progressed rapidly since the days of the vacuum tube, but the field of computing would not be where it is today without this important invention. Building on the transistor, the integrated circuit was born, and today, this technology is found in numerous devices, from desktop computers to video game consoles. But science is never content to rest on its laurels; new discoveries are always being made in an effort to create better and more efficient electronics.

Personal Computers (PCs): The 1970 s and 1980 s saw the rise of personal computers (PCs), making computing accessible to individuals and businesses. The invention of the graphical user interface (GUI) in the 1980 s made computers easier to use for non-technical users.

TYPES OF COMPUTERS:
 

Computers come in various types, each designed for specific purposes and catering to different needs. The main types of computers are:

Personal Computers (PCs):

Desktops:  These computers are designed to sit on a desk and consist of a separate monitor, keyboard, and processing unit. They are versatile and suitable for a wide range of tasks.
Laptops (Notebooks): Portable computers with integrated components, including a screen, keyboard, and track pad or mouse. Laptops are suitable for on-the-go computing.
Servers:

Server Computers: Optimized for handling and managing network resources, servers provide services to other computers on a network. They often operate without a monitor or keyboard, with administrators managing them remotely.
Mainframes:

Mainframe Computers: Powerful and large-scale machines designed for handling extensive amounts of data and supporting numerous users simultaneously. Mainframes are commonly used in large organizations for critical business applications.
Supercomputers:

Supercomputers: Extremely powerful machines built to process vast amounts of data and perform complex calculations at extraordinary speeds. Supercomputers are used for scientific research, weather modeling, simulations, and other compute-intensive tasks.
Embedded Systems:

Embedded Computers: Compact computers integrated into other devices or systems, designed for specific functions. Examples include the embedded systems in cars, household appliances, and industrial machines.
Tablets and Smartphones:

Tablets and Smartphones: Mobile devices equipped with computing capabilities. They have touchscreens and are designed for portability, offering a wide range of applications for communication, productivity, and entertainment.
Wearable Computers:

Wearable Computers: Small computing devices integrated into clothing or accessories. Examples include smartwatches and fitness trackers, which provide features beyond traditional timekeeping or health monitoring.
Game Consoles:

Game Consoles: Specialized computers designed for gaming. They come with dedicated hardware for rendering graphics and processing game-specific tasks. Examples include PlayStation, Xbox, and Nintendo consoles.
Workstations:

Workstation Computers: High-performance computers tailored for professional tasks such as graphic design, video editing, and engineering. They offer enhanced processing power and graphics capabilities.
Thin Clients:

Thin Clients: Computers that rely on a central server for processing and storage. They are lightweight and mainly used for accessing applications and data from a centralized source.
Quantum Computers:

Quantum Computers: Experimental computers that leverage the principles of quantum mechanics to perform computations using qubits. Quantum computers have the potential to solve certain problems exponentially faster than classical computers.
